ABSTRACT The surge in access to explicit content across various platforms has sparked major concerns, yet
existing content filtering systems find it difficult to analyze different media formats leading to the spread of
unchecked dissemination of harmful content. To tackle these shortcomings, the authors proposed SHIELD,
which is an optimized end-to-end pipeline to detect & analyze explicit content, using a large-languagemodel (LLM) driven approach. SHIELD processes multi media inputs by segregating and preprocessing
them, followed by converting all formats into text through advanced models, extracting meaningful textual
context and subjecting the resulting data to two parallel evaluation mechanisms: an LLM-based classifier for
contextual analysis, and a semantic vector-based scoring system for quantitative measurement. Explicitness
classifications are output in a JSON format, which allows easy integration into real-world systems. When
benchmarked against a manually curated ground truth dataset, the LLM-based system surpasses vector-based
approach, with an accuracy of 93.32%, as against 67.81%. The pipeline shows robustness across all media
types and file sizes, confirming its viability as a scalable, context-aware solution.
INDEX TERMS Explicit content detection, large language model (LLM), vector embeddings, content
moderation, multimodal content analysis.
I. INTRODUCTION

With the quick and uncontrolled spread of explicit content
on the internet, a serious societal problem arises. While the
internet provides endless avenues to information and global
connectivity, it simultaneously facilitates the widespread
dissemination of harmful and explicit content. Much of
the exposure is unintentional, especially for children and
teenagers, a situation worsened by the enormous amount
of digital content created every day and the inadequacy of
traditional filtering mechanisms. Psychological and social
effects of such exposure are much profound and require
careful analysis.
Studies show that even incidental exposure to explicit content can sensitize adolescents to violence and
inappropriate behavior, with correlations between such

A proposed system for explicit content detection would
handle various inputs: audio, video, and text. First, a content splitter would categorize the input. Audio would be
transcribed into text, and all text would then be cleaned
and prepared for analysis. This text is converted into
numerical data (vector embeddings) that the systems can
process. Video files would be checked for corruption and
standardized.
At the core, specialized LLMs would analyze the prepared
text from audio and video sources. These models would
perform sophisticated classification to identify different
categories of explicit content, such as sexually explicit
material or promotions of self-harm. The system would then
calculate the percentage of the content that falls into each
category, providing a detailed statistical breakdown. Finally,
all these findings are synthesized into a clear, practical report
for content analysis.
The primary objectives of this research are:
1. To develop a robust system capable of detecting explicit
content across multiple media types, including audio,
video, images, and text.
2. To integrate different preprocessing and processing
techniques for each media type, ensuring that the system
can handle diverse input formats.
3. To leverage large language models (LLMs) for nuanced,
context-aware detection and scoring of explicit content
as it moves through the pipeline.
4. To benchmark the system’s performance by comparing
it with traditional vector-based approach on realworld datasets ensuring, efficiency in detecting explicit
content.

II. RELATED WORK

input. Their method is based on modality-specific processing

and then classified. Although successful in rough-grained
explicit content detection, the framework has been shown
to have constraints when dealing with fine-grained video

A. RECENT MULTIMODAL CONTENT MODERATION
SYSTEMS

Multimodal content moderation systems combining visual,
textual, and auditory signals to identify harmful or explicit
content have become the focus of recent research because
of the rising number of cross-modal hazards, including hate
memes or videos with conflicting visual and audio information. Traditional unimodal systems often fail in these settings,
prompting the development of model-centric multimodal
fusion architectures that tightly couple modality-specific
encoders through early, late, or cross-attention-based fusion
strategies. Representative approaches include VideoModerator, which integrates visual, audio, and OCR signals
for domain-specific video moderation [28], Asymmetric
Mixed-Modal Moderation (AM3), which explicitly models
semantic asymmetry across modalities using contrastive
learning [36], and MM-HSD, which employs cross-modal
attention to fuse speech, visual frames, and on-screen
text for hate speech detection in videos [37]. Other
systems such as Hi-Guard [38], ShieldVLM [39], and
ToxVidLM [40] further extend multimodal moderation by
incorporating policy hierarchies, implicit toxicity modeling,
and multilingual reasoning, while evaluation benchmarks
like MM-SafetyBench assess the robustness of multimodal
large language models against unsafe manipulations in paired
image–text inputs [41]. While these systems demonstrate
strong performance on task-specific benchmarks, they are
typically built around tightly coupled multimodal encoders
and fixed fusion architectures, requiring modality-aligned
supervision and retraining to incorporate new input types
or evolving moderation policies. This architectural rigidity limits their adaptability in heterogeneous, real world
moderation pipelines where arbitrary combinations of text,
image, audio, and video content must be handled consistently.
In contrast to this, SHIELD adopts a pipeline centric,
semantic normalization strategy; rather than an end-to-end
multimodal fusion model. Heterogeneous inputs, including
text, images, audio, and video, are first transformed into a
unified textual abstraction through modality-specific preprocessing such as transcription, captioning, and summarization.
This normalized semantic representation is subsequently
analyzed using both LLM-based contextual reasoning and
vector-based similarity scoring. By decoupling modality
handling from semantic evaluation, SHIELD emphasizes
modularity, extensibility, and interpretability, enabling consistent moderation across arbitrary media types without
retraining multimodal encoders. Consequently, SHIELD
complements existing fusion-based systems by providing a
general-purpose, architecture-agnostic moderation backbone
focused on generalized policy reasoning and explicitness
assessment, rather than competing directly with specialized
multimodal classifiers.

III. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY

According to the analyzed literature, even though previous studies like Bhatti et al. [9] have tried multimodal
explicit-content detection among text, audio, and video, such
methods still have significant shortcomings. Specifically,
current systems handle the different types of media separately
and do not combine the contextual information across media.
Moreover, there is no previous system that collectively
examines mixed media inputs or makes use of cross-modal
semantic dependencies (e.g. matching audio transcripts to
video cues or matching textual and visual signals within the
same content). Thus, the literature gap is not in the form of
absence of multimodal systems, but in the sense that a single
context-sensitive pipeline that can process heterogeneous
inputs and integrate their semantic contributions to more
robust explicit-content detection has just not been learned
yet. This research proposes a holistic framework for explicit
content detection that deals with the simultaneous processing
and analysis of multiple types of media. The proposed
methodology for explicit content detection is designed to
handle multiple media types, including audio, video, image,
and text files. The system first accepts input in the form of
a folder containing mixed media files. These files are then
categorized into distinct types: audio, video, image, and text;
creating specific data objects for each. Data objects serve to
handle as well as to store media contents at each pipeline
stage. For the preprocessing stage, the system conducts
checks against corruption and ensures standard formats are
maintained for different types of media. Audio data has
its background noise filtered, and its format is normalized.
Video files are extracted and their resolutions adjusted. Image
files are resized and pixel values are standardized. Text files
are tokenized and cleaned of any unnecessary characters.
During the processing stage, every media-type is fed into a
respective model for processing. Audio files are transcribed
using the Groq Whisper V3 Turbo. Video content is processed
using Google’s Gemini Pro to extract contextual information.
Images are analyzed using the Groq LLama 3 Vision for
image-to-context conversion, while text data is used as it
is. These analysed results are stored in corresponding data
objects. Finally, explicit content is scored both by LLM-based
and Vector-based scoring. LLM offers a context-aware score
whereas vector embeddings are used to determine the cosine
similarity with explicit content samples.

analytical processes. With the data already pre-processed,

every one of the modules yields its own explicitness score,

performance. The Figure 2 illustrates the complete pipeline in

B. MODEL SELECTION RATIONALE

In this work, the Llama-3.1-70B model has been chosen
because it has the most advanced performance on four levels,
including contextual understanding, safety classification, and
long-range semantic reasoning. Being a foundation model
that is trained on various multilingual and multimodal
data, Llama-3.1-70B has high generalization and much
better instruction-following behavior than other previous
LLMs. These attributes make it specifically well adapted to
explicit-content detection, where sensitive contextual data,

VOLUME 14, 2026

implicit indicators of harm, and cross-modal alignment of
semantics are needed to offer reliable classification. Its
superior safety-tuning also offers resilience in accordance
to hallucinations and misclassification in case of sensitive
content categories. The vector-based baseline was selected
based on an all-MiniLM-L6-v2 embedding model because
it is efficient, has a lightweight architecture, and achieves
a stable and high performance in semantic similarity
tasks. Being a distilled transformer encoder, MiniLM-L6v2 supports sentence embeddings of high quality at just
a fraction of the computational costs of larger embedding
models, providing fast inference and scalable vector space
comparisons. This renders it the right representative of the
traditional embedding-based pipelines, and it is possible to
rightly compare the classical semantic-retrieval methods with

D. ANNOTATION DESIGN AND GUIDELINES

The ground truth annotation procedure was aimed at ensuring
semantic consistency, reliability, and interpretability across
multimodal content. Each sample was rated by several
annotators using a continuous explicitness scale, which
recorded subtle degrees of harmfulness beyond binary
classification. The design decision is directly in line with
the goal of SHIELD to generate fined-grained explicitness
scores and not just coarse labels. Blind annotation was done
to the model output to avoid confirmation or anchoring bias.
The disagreements between the annotators were resolved
in a formalized consensus process. Conflicting labels were
reviewed jointly, and final annotations were determined via
discussion guided strictly by the annotation protocol.
Annotators were provided with a comprehensive written
set of guidelines with clearly defined categories of content,
severity definitions, contextual concerns (implicit, suggestive, and borderline cases) and modality specific problems.
Such structured annotation schemas are critical for aligning
human judgment in moderation datasets, as highlighted in
prior work emphasizing their impact on annotation reliability
and consistency. The severity scoring protocol defined five
ranges: 0% indicated the category was completely absent
with no indication of relevant content; 1-25% indicated mild
or implied presence such as suggestive language, innuendo,
or indirect references; 26-50% indicated moderate presence
where content was clearly present but not extreme, such
as profanity or mild violence depictions; 51-75% indicated
significant presence with strong, overt content including
explicit language or graphic descriptions; and 76-100%
indicated extreme or graphic presence with highly explicit
visual or audio depictions representing severe violations.
Annotators applied contextual judgment rather than relying

on surface cues, beginning with an assessment of intent
to distinguish educational or documentary material from

1) INTER-ANNOTATOR AGREEMENT ANALYSIS

Methodology and Rationale: To quantify the reliability and
consistency of the ground truth annotation process, interannotator agreement (IAA) was assessed using Krippendorff’s alpha (α) and the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient
(ICC), both of which are widely accepted measures for ordinal and continuous annotation tasks. These metrics evaluate
whether annotations reflect reproducible semantic judgments
rather than subjective interpretation, ensuring that the dataset
provides a reliable evaluation benchmark for multimodal
content moderation. Given the three-annotator setup and
continuous severity scores (0–100%), two complementary
IAA metrics were employed. Krippendorff’s alpha (α) was
selected for its suitability for multiple annotators, ability to
handle interval-scale data, correction for chance agreement,
and robustness to missing values and variable sample sizes.
According to established benchmarks (Krippendorff, 2004),
α ≥ 0.67 supports tentative conclusions, while α ≥
0.80 enables definitive conclusions.
The Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) was used as a
complementary metric, specifically model ICC(2,k), which
evaluates absolute agreement among the mean ratings of
the three annotators. This model is well-suited for assessing
consistency of continuous severity scores. Interpretation
guidelines from Cicchetti (1994) classify ICC values below
0.40 as poor, 0.40–0.59 as fair, 0.60–0.74 as good, and values
of 0.75 or higher as excellent.

J. VECTOR-BASED SCORING

high-dimensional vector space and comparing it to a filtered

the underlying semantics of the content. This is based on

K. MODEL PERFORMANCE EVALUATION SETUP

In this paper, we ran benchmarking over a manually gathered
dataset with more than 6,000 items that included 1,500 items
of each of the four types of media: audio, video, image, and
text. Benchmarking was run on an 8 GB RAM simple setup
with an Intel 10th Gen i3 CPU. We used this as a setup to test
the performance of the new LLM-based pipeline compared to
currently employed vector-based pipeline for explicit content
detection on various media types. The main goal of this
benchmarking task was to determine how effectively the
LLM-based method surpasses the vector-based pipeline in
accuracy. To enable this, a ground truth dataset of 6,000
mixed data items was manually prepared. This ground truth
acted the standard for measuring the accuracy of both
pipelines. Through comparison of both systems’ output with
the manually labeled ground truth, we were able to quantify
and compare their performance.
The results obtained, were collected systematically and
organized in a tabular format, in a CSV file, for thorough
analysis. Each entry in the CSV corresponds to a unique
file checked for several explicit content categories per media
type, i.e., images (IMG), audio (AUDIO), and video (VID).
For each pair of file and category, the table contains the
predicted scores computed by both pipelines (LLMScore
and Vector-Score), the human-labeled Ground-Truth score
(between 0 and 100), and binary labels obtained from
predefined classification thresholds (GT Label, LLM Label,
Vector Label). Further metadata like file name, media type,
model inference time (LLM-Time, Vector-Time), and file size
was also tracked to judge efficiency in addition to accuracy.

C. STATISTICAL VALIDATION AND ROBUSTNESS

To assess whether the observed performance improvements of SHIELD over the baseline are robust to sampling variability, statistical validation was conducted using
bootstrap-based confidence interval estimation. Bootstrap
resampling is a distribution-free technique that is widely used
in supervised learning evaluation when repeated experimental
runs or full predictive distributions are unavailable. This
approach enables estimation of metric uncertainty and
stability while avoiding assumptions about underlying data
distributions.

D. Kapoor et al.: SHIELD: System for Harmful Explicit-Content Identification and Evaluation

Specifically, 95% confidence intervals (CI) were computed for key evaluation metrics by generating 1,000
bootstrap resamples of the test set with replacement and
re-evaluating both pipelines on each iteration. This procedure
provides an empirical assessment of variability across
resampled datasets and supports robust comparison between
methods.
The bootstrap analysis confirms the stability and robustness of SHIELD’s performance across metrics. For accuracy,
SHIELD achieved a mean score of 93.32% with a 95% CI of
[92.1%, 94.4%], whereas the vector-based baseline achieved
67.81% with a 95% CI of [65.5%, 70.1%]. Similarly,
SHIELD attained an F1-score of 0.84 (95% CI: [0.82, 0.86]),
substantially exceeding the baseline F1-score of 0.55 (95%
CI: [0.52, 0.58]). Comparable stability trends were observed
for error-based metrics such as MAE and MSE.
Importantly, the confidence intervals for SHIELD and the
baseline exhibit no overlap across the evaluated metrics,
indicating that the observed performance differences are
unlikely to arise from sampling variability alone. This
provides strong statistical evidence that the improvements
introduced by LLM-based semantic reasoning are systematic and robust, rather than incidental effects of dataset
composition. Given the scale of evaluation involving over
6,000 multimodal samples and the consistency of gains
across content categories and modalities, the observed
reductions in error and variance reflect practically meaningful
improvements in moderation performance. The application of
additional parametric inferential tests is identified as future
work as more fine-grained per-sample experimental records
are maintained.

supporting context-sensitive modulation strategies such as
severity tiering, adaptive filtering, and scaled prioritization.
The regression results also indicate another semantic layer in

LLMs would also be more advantageous for binary detection
problems but also much more qualified in the environment as
regards interpretability, granularity, and semantic value are
of main concern. Both in its discrete classifying form and its
continuous regression form, the LLM pipeline is the more
sensitive, more reliable, and future-proof way to explicitly
detecting content in dynamic and morally ambiguous media
spheres.

B. COMPUTATIONAL COST AND RESOURCE
REQUIREMENTS

The proposed system is characterized by the heavy computational cost due to the use of large language models and
auxiliary features of audio, text, and multimodal processing.
Modern LLMs with high parameter counts incur non-trivial
inference costs, both in terms of API pricing and GPU
resource consumption, which directly affects scalability
and practical deployment. To perform text and context
analysis, the system uses the Llama-3.3-70B model at a
cost of $0.59 per million input tokens and 0.79 per million
output tokens. Although individual inferences may appear
inexpensive, high processing rates quickly add to the total
cost, in particular in the setting when the volume of content
can reach to millions of samples per day. Its bulky parameter
size also requires expensive hardware (e.g., A100/H100
GPUs in on-premise deployment) which adds to the cost
of operation. Given that multimedia content often consists
of audio segments, the cost of transcription may increase
in direct proportion to dataset size. This component is
a major contribution in recurring operation expenses in
real-time moderation environments where the continuous
audio streams are to be processed. Gemini 1.5 pro-002,
primarily used for video processing, has another price
structure, which involves text, image, and video pricing at
$0.10 per million input tokens, and audio pricing at $0.70 per
million tokens, in addition to output price of $0.40 per
million output tokens. Although the costs of the model to
work with text-based workloads are favorable, it costs more
to execute audio and context-caching tasks, which adds to
the total cost when using the model in multimodal analysis
of batches and repeated processes. All in all there is an
evident trade off between the cost of computation and the
level of accuracy and operational efficiency. LLM-based
scoring provides better contextual information at the cost of
higher financial and hardware requirements, as opposed to
more computationally cheap, yet less accurate, scoring based
on vector based. For large-scale or continuous moderation
platforms, this necessitates careful engineering decisions,
such as selective invocation of LLMs, batching strategies,
model distillation, or hybrid scoring, to maintain feasibility
without compromising detection quality.

IX. CONCLUSION

The paper offers a detailed discourse of an LLM-based
solution in comparison with a vector-based solution to the
problem of detecting explicit content on diverse types of
media. The implicit message is that the LLM pipeline is
more reliable, adaptable, and contextual compared to the
alternative pipeline, based on vector. While vector-based
solutions could be pretty reliable with single types of media
but pale in comparison to LLM-based pipeline with mixed
data due to their reliance on word embeddings and limited
capability of making sense of the information of contexts.
On the other hand, the LLM-based pipeline is superior as
it incorporates a deep contextual perspective of all media
inputs of a text, image, audio, or video with the aim of
making more reliable and precise predictions in a spectrum of
diverse content types. This methodology highlights the need
for contextual knowledge in enhancing the effectiveness of
content detection systems. Context-aware analysis is a significant feature of content detection as analysed. The pipeline
based on the LLM manages the process of each type of
media in the knowledge of the contexts, yielding a consistent
and highly precise process with a diverse spectrum of types
of media as well as of diverse content sizes. Subjecting
each type of content to semantic awareness, the LLM-based
solution ensures high-end performance regardless of the
format or complexity and marks an improved development
compared to the typical vector-based solution. Finally, the
LLM-based solution surpasses the earlier methods based
on vectors because it ensures more precise and reliable
explicit material detection as well as regard for the content

A. LIMITATIONS

Despite the high contextual performance observed in the
proposed SHIELD framework with respect to a range of
media inputs, a number of limitations still exist. The analysis
is based on the quality and accessibility of the underlying
dataset, which cannot possibly capture the broad range of
harmful material found on the actual platforms. In addition,
the pipeline implemented on the LLM is highly accurate but
intensive to implement in terms of computational complexity
in inference, thus limiting its feasibility as a first-stage filter
in resource-constrained deployments. The system also has
dependency on pretrained models whose performance can
vary depending on domain changes or vague contents. These
aspects indicate where the existing work can be reinforced.

The SHIELD pipeline can also be extended in the future

APPENDIX A
A. IMPLEMENTATION ASSUMPTIONS, SCALABILITY, AND
REPRODUCIBILITY
1) LLM EXECUTION ASSUMPTIONS

SHIELD uses large language models (LLMs) as semantic
reasoning modules instead of using them as overall classifiers. All LLM invocations use fixed prompt templates,
documented model versions, and deterministic decoding
settings (e.g., low temperature) to minimize stochastic
variability. This design aligns with best practices in reproducible research practices, where controlling prompting and
decoding parameters is essential for achieving a consistent behavior. Importantly, LLM usage within SHIELD is
modular and replaceable at the interface level: alternative
LLMs or embedding models can be substituted without
altering the core pipeline logic, requiring only prompt-level
or configuration-level adjustments rather than architectural
changes.
2) SCALABILITY CONSIDERATIONS

The SHIELD pipeline is explicitly designed for scalable
deployment. Modality-specific preprocessing stages, such
as speech-to-text, OCR, and visual frame captioning, can
be batched, parallelized, and distributed independently of
the semantic reasoning stage. Computationally intensive
components can thus be scaled horizontally, while LLM
inference can be rate-limited, cached, or reused for repeated
or similar content. This architectural decoupling mirrors
strategies adopted in large-scale moderation systems to
29519

D. Kapoor et al.: SHIELD: System for Harmful Explicit-Content Identification and Evaluation

balance throughput, latency, and semantic depth, enabling
SHIELD to operate over large content streams with controllable cost and performance.
3) REPRODUCIBILITY

All preprocessing steps, semantic normalization procedures,
explicitness scoring mechanisms, and evaluation metrics
are fully specified. Dataset splits, annotation protocols,
prompt templates, and model configurations are documented
to support reproducibility. While certain components rely
on third-party models or APIs, SHIELD’s modular design
ensures that functionally equivalent open-source alternatives
can be substituted through configuration or prompt-level
changes without altering the core methodology. To further
support reproducible experimentation, preprocessing modules, scoring functions, prompt templates, and evaluation
scripts will be documented or released upon publication,
enabling replication and extension across datasets and model
configurations.
